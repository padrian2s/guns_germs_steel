Summary

Acute epidemic childhood diseases: measles, rubella, mumps, pertussis, smallpox. Rapid microbe spread and rapid symptom course creates epidemic patterns. Local populations quickly infected. Result: death or recovery-immunity. Rapid cycling exhausts susceptible population then disease disappears (requires reintroduction). Geographic population size determines epidemic sustainability.

Key Concepts

Epidemic diseases require threshold population size. High transmission rate + rapid immunity = epidemic burn-out pattern. Measles example: must infect enough people quickly to ensure spread. Small populations: insufficient susceptible pool. Disease dies out locally. Critical population size: determines epidemic disease persistence.

Historical & Geographic Context

Measles requires 200,000+ population for endemic persistence (historical calculation). European cities: sufficient density enabled endemic measles. Americas indigenous villages: typically <5,000 people (insufficient for endemic measles). Measles introduced to Americas: rapid epidemic then disappearance (insufficient population for endemic cycle). Old World civilization size: supported epidemic childhood diseases evolution. Americas settlement patterns: too small for endemic epidemic disease evolution.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic agriculture enabled dense settlement. Dense settlement exceeded critical epidemic population threshold. Epidemics endemic in large populations. Americas settlements smaller: below epidemic threshold. Old World dense populations evolved all childhood epidemics. Americas sparse populations: lacked endemic epidemic diseases. Population immunity patterns geography-determined. When populations met: Old World carried endemic epidemics. Americas lacked immunity to dense-population evolved diseases.
