Summary

Horse herd social structure enables domestication. Hierarchical organization: ranking female leads, followed by foals (age order), other mares ranked, each with foals. Dominance hierarchy prevents constant fighting; each member knows rank. Social structure perfect for domestication: humans replace top rank, animals follow human leadership naturally.

Key Concepts

Social structure genetics determine domestication. Hierarchical herd structure enables dominance hierarchy adoption by humans. Animals naturally follow human as they would natural leader. Non-hierarchical species (territorial antelopes) cannot form human-led groups. Social genetics cascade to domestication feasibility.

Historical & Geographic Context

Horse herds naturally ranked through female dominance, inheritance of rank through foals. Genetic social structure evolved on Eurasian steppes. Humans exploit existing hierarchy structure: lead animals follow human as surrogate leader. Domestic horse packs maintain behavioral pattern from wild ancestry.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic fauna behavioral genetics determine domestication feasibility. Horse genetic social structure enables human dominance integration. Geographic fauna lacking hierarchical genetics (territorial antelopes, zebras) cannot accommodate human authority. Eurasia provided hierarchical fauna; Americas/Africa provided non-hierarchical fauna. Behavioral genetics geography-distributed determine domestication possibility.
