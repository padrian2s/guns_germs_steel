Summary

Bibliography on African pastoralism and sub-Saharan agriculture: research on food production development, Saharan plant exploitation (8,000 years BP), pastoralism origins and geographic spread across Africa. Madagascar culture history research: Austronesian settlement reaching African island. Comprehensive research documenting African geographic agricultural diversity and Indian Ocean maritime connections.

Key Concepts

African pastoralism: animal domestication (cattle, sheep, goats) adapted to grassland geography enabling pastoralist societies. Saharan plant use: early exploitation preceding agriculture. Sub-Saharan agriculture: independent crop domestication reflecting climate/geographic constraints. Geographic diversity: African agriculture reflecting region-specific domestication patterns.

Historical & Geographic Context

Sub-Saharan agriculture development: millet, sorghum, yam domestication ~5000 years BP independent of Old World Fertile Crescent. Pastoralism development: ~7000 years BP independent emergence in Saharan grasslands. Saharan plant use: ~8,000 years ago documented exploitation predating domestication. Madagascar settlement: Austronesian seafarers reaching island ~1500 years ago establishing Indian Ocean connection. Geographic distances: Africa-Madagascar maritime expansion required advanced navigation technology.

Connection to Main Thesis

African agriculture: independent origin reflecting geographic domesticable species availability. Pastoralism development: geographic grassland regions enabled animal domestication technology development. Saharan plant exploitation: geographic resource abundance supporting early human investment. Madagascar settlement: geographic island position enabled Austronesian maritime technology application. African-Indian Ocean: geographic maritime connections enabling technology diffusion despite distances. Geographic diversity determines African agricultural/pastoral systems: no unified continental technology but region-specific adaptations reflecting geographic habitat differences.
