Summary

Large wild mammal scarcity constrains domestication options. Globally: only ~148 species of large wild terrestrial herbivores/omnivores potentially domesticable. Far fewer than plant species available. Modest number of factors determine domestication suitability. Straightforward to test regional mammal availability explaining domestication absence.

Key Concepts

Limited mammal domestication candidates globally. Herbivore/omnivore requirement eliminating carnivores. Size requirement limiting small species. Behavioral requirement (not aggressive, herding). Growth speed requirement (years not decades). Fertility requirement (multiple births). Genetic traits determining domesticability narrow candidate pool severely.

Historical & Geographic Context

Eurasia: large herbivores available (horses, cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, camels, yaks). Africa: limited to cattle, few others. Americas: llama/alpaca only South America, no others. Australia: no large domesticable mammals. Geographic mammal distribution determined which regions could develop animal agriculture/transport/power.

Connection to Main Thesis

Large mammal availability geography-limited. Eurasian dominance in domesticable species (14 of 14 Ancient Fourteen): advantage in transport, agriculture, military, disease. Americas/Australia absence: agricultural limitations, no plow power, no large domestic animals for military. Geographic mammal distribution directly determines civilization capacity for technology, conquest, disease development.
