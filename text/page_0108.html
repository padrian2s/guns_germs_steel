Summary

Forager optimization strategy: maximize calorie return, minimize time/effort, reduce starvation risk. Available species determine which strategy optimal. Regions with reliable wild foods support hunting-gathering indefinitely. Regions with declining wild foods force agriculture adoption despite higher labor costs and health decline.

Key Concepts

Rational economic behavior within geographic constraints. Foragers choose optimally given available options. Agriculture adoption forced by carrying capacity limits, not preference. Population growth from any cause forces agriculture when wild foods insufficient.

Historical & Geographic Context

Post-glacial climate change made some wild foods scarcer. Megafauna extinction eliminated large game. Population continued growing. Regions unable to support foragers shifted to agriculture. Regions with continued wild abundance (California, Australia in some areas) remained foraging.

Connection to Main Thesis

Rational human behavior explains agricultural adoption: forced not chosen. Geographic wild food carrying capacity determines whether adoption necessary. Regions with permanent wild abundance never adopt agriculture. Regions facing resource decline forced adoption. Geography, via carrying capacity, determines trajectory.
