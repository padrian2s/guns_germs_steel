Summary

Population replacement versus cultural diffusion. Austronesian expansion (Philippines, Indonesia) and Bantu expansion (sub-Saharan Africa) involved population replacement - new peoples replacing hunter-gatherers. Skeletal evidence shows morphological difference. Agricultural diffusion can occur via new peoples replacing old, or via local adoption. Geographic and genetic evidence distinguishes patterns.

Key Concepts

Two agricultural spread mechanisms: diffusion (local adoption) versus demic expansion (population replacement). Austronesian and Bantu: replacement patterns with new peoples bringing agriculture. Mediterranean/Atlantic: adoption patterns without replacement. Geographic barriers and population densities determine which pattern occurs.

Historical & Geographic Context

Austronesian expansion ~4000 BP: Agricultural populations from South China colonized Philippines, Indonesia, eventually Pacific. Bantu expansion ~3000 BP: Agricultural populations from West Africa colonized sub-Saharan Africa. Both displacement patterns visible archaeologically through pottery changes and skeletal replacement. Different from Mediterranean adoption patterns.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic context determines population replacement versus adoption. Island environments (Philippines, Indonesia) enabled agricultural replacement. Vast continents (Africa) enabled Bantu expansion and local replacement. Geographic scale and barriers determine whether agriculture spreads by diffusion or by population movement. Geographic factors determine which peoples end up with agricultural advantage.
