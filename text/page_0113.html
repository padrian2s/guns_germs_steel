Summary

Hunter-gatherers persist geographically isolated from farmers. California natives: separated by deserts from Arizona farmers. Cape Khoisan: separated from Bantu farmers by incompatible crop zones. Aboriginal Australians: separated by narrow seas from Indonesian/New Guinea farmers. Geographic barriers protecting hunter-gatherers against agricultural encroachment.

Key Concepts

Geographic isolation enables hunter-gatherer persistence. Incompatible crop zones prevent diffusion. Mountain, desert, sea barriers protect foragers. Agricultural expansion limits geographic: climatic, crop-specific. Buffered regions maintain hunter-gatherer populations indefinitely.

Historical & Geographic Context

California-Arizona deserts separated hunter-gatherer from farmer populations for millennia. Mediterranean Cape isolated Khoisan from Bantu agricultural expansion. Australia geographic isolation maintained hunter-gatherers until European contact despite agriculture development in nearby Indonesia/Papua.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic barriers determine agricultural expansion limits. Some populations geographically protected from agricultural competition. Agricultural technology irrelevant without domesticable species in new regions. Deserts, mountains, incompatible climates preserve pre-agricultural societies. Geography determines which peoples agricultural, which remain hunter-gatherers.
