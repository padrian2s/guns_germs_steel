Summary

Natural experiment methodology: cannot perform controlled experiments on human societies. Instead study "natural experiments" where similar conditions naturally occurred. Polynesia settlement provides ideal example: diverse island environments, same founding population colonizing all, allowing study of environmental effects on human adaptation.

Key Concepts

Natural experiment advantages: common ancestors, similar starting technology/culture, diverse resulting environments. Controls for cultural/genetic variation by holding both constant, varying only geography. Polynesian colonization from Bismarck Archipelago ~1200 BCE onward populated diverse islands to A.D. 1000.

Historical & Geographic Context

Polynesian colonization pattern: ~1200 BCE onward from single source population. Moriori-Maori collision miniature test within medium-sized Polynesian test. Different island environments selected for different adaptive strategies.

Connection to Main Thesis

Polynesia tests geographic determinism hypothesis with controlled ancestry/technology, varied geographies. If geography drives societal development, Polynesian diversity should reflect environmental variation. Sets stage for detailed environmental analysis determining societal complexity.
