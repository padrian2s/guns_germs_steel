Summary

Eastern U.S. Native Americans developed agriculture locally (from local domesticates), supported sedentary villages, and developed cultural florescence (Hopewell culture 200 BCE-400 CE). Possessed thousands of years to exploit available wild plants for potential crops. Geographic constraints determined available species, not human effort.

Key Concepts

Domesticable species availability geographic-determined. Hopewell agriculture supported villages and florescence: evidence of adequate agricultural base. Limited species available in eastern U.S. flora limited agricultural expansion beyond adopted domesticates. Geographyâ€”not effort or capabilityâ€”determined species ceiling.

Historical & Geographic Context

Hopewell culture 200 BCE-400 CE: sedentary villages, food storage, craft specialization, cultural florescence (mounds, artifacts). Agricultural base: maize, beans, squash domesticated thousands of years prior. Indigenous population demonstrated thousands-of-years timespan for adopting other crops if available. Yet agriculture never expanded beyond existing domesticates.

Connection to Main Thesis

Hopewell success with existing crops proves geographic determinism explicitly. Population had ample time (millennia) and demonstrated capability (florescence achieved). Yet no additional crops domesticated. Available wild species in eastern U.S. lacked domesticability. Geography's flora distribution determined adoption ceiling independent of human factors.
