Summary

New Guinea agriculture emerged independently around 7000 BCE, contemporary with Fertile Crescent transition. Archaeological evidence shows domestication of root crops (taro, yams) in New Guinea highlands while surrounding regions remained hunter-gatherer. Geographic isolation enabled independent agricultural development.

Key Concepts

Independent agricultural origins geography-determined. New Guinea: available root crops (taro, yams) enabled domestication. Surrounding regions lacked equivalent domesticable species, remained hunter-gatherers. Same timeframe as Fertile Crescent shows agriculture emerged independently when suitable species available.

Historical & Geographic Context

New Guinea 7000 BCE: agriculture emerged from taro, yam domestication. Fertile Crescent 10000 BCE: wheat, barley, livestock domestication. Two independent centers ~3000 years apart. Different species availability determined timing difference. Americas maize domestication ~5000 BCE even later. Geographic species distribution determines agricultural timing globally.

Connection to Main Thesis

New Guinea independent agriculture proves geographic determinism. Same continents, different regions showed independent agricultural emergence when suitable species available. Geographic distribution of wild domesticable species determined agricultural transition timing. Earlier species availability (Fertile Crescent wheat) enabled earlier transition. Later species availability (Americas maize) enabled later transition. Geography's species distribution determines agriculture emergence timing.
