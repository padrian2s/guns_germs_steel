Rezumat

Two case studies demonstrate geographic determinism in crop domestication. Different regions' indigenous crops reflect geography-available species. Some domesticable species remain unused due to geographic availability differences. Case studies illuminate why certain geographic areas succeeded agriculturally while others lagged.

Concepte Cheie

Geography determines crop availability. Indigenous crops differ by region based on geographic flora. Some regions possess superior domesticable crops, others inferior. Domestication success depends on available species genetics (domesticability traits), not human innovation.

Chapter's case studies compare crop domestication across regions. Fertile Crescent: large-seeded grasses, pulses enabled dense agriculture. Mesoamerica: maize, beans, squash enabled agriculture despite lower productivity. New Guinea: root crops enabled agriculture within constraints. Each region's crops reflect geography-available wild species.
