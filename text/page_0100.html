Summary

Some regions domesticated multiple local species, others received "founder" crops/animals from elsewhere, enabling sedentary settlement and local agriculture development. Imported domesticates catalyzed local domestication of remaining wild species. Geographic spread of farming facilitated by these founder species establishing agricultural base.

Key Concepts

Founder crops/animals enable transition to sedentary food production. Agricultural diffusion via founder domesticates. Imported crops establish agricultural system, enabling local crop domestication from wild plants previously unsuitable for farming. Founder species determine agriculture feasibility in new regions.

Historical & Geographic Context

Sub-Saharan Africa: millet, sorghum from founder crops diffused from Sahel. India: rice agriculture built on founder crops diffused from China. Europe: founder crops from Near East enabled European agriculture expansion northward. Japan, Americas: founder domesticates enabled regional agricultural specialization.

Connection to Main Thesis

Agricultural diffusion depends on geographic proximity and climate similarity. Founder crops succeed in similar climates, enabling agriculture adoption. Regions geographically distant from agricultural centers develop agriculture later/never. Geographic barriers limit founder crop diffusion. Isolation determines whether agriculture adopted locally versus received as diffusion.
