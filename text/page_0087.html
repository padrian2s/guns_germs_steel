Summary

Agricultural societies enabled dense population supporting specialists and hierarchies. Food surplus allowed elites to emerge, armies to form, bureaucracies to develop. Disease evolution from domestic animals provided additional conquest advantage: Eurasian livestock diseases killed 95% of Americas populations.

Key Concepts

Food surplus â†’ political organization. Hunter-gatherers egalitarian by necessity (everyone hunts). Farmers: food stockpiling allows elite control, taxation, hierarchy. Sedentary settlement required by agriculture generates urban disease. Livestock germs evolve into human pathogens in dense agricultural populations.

Historical & Geographic Context

Chapter begins mapping agricultural transition globally. Fertile Crescent (sheep, goats, cattle, pigs, horses) generated dense societies and epidemic diseases by 1000 BCE. Americas lacked comparable domesticated animals for disease evolution.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic variation in domesticable animals explains disease divergence. Eurasian domesticated animals (cattle, pigs, sheep) transmitted pathogens to human populations living in proximity. Americas lacked equivalent domesticates, so Americans never developed immunity to Old World diseases. This geographic fact decided conquest outcome more than military technology.
