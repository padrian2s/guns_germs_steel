Summary

New Guinea agricultural limitations: Indigenous food production limited despite available land. New Guinea tribes adopted introduced crops (sweet potato, new livestock) enthusiastically when available. Tribes with preferential access to introduced crops expand versus those without. Key Concepts

Geographic constraints on indigenous agriculture. New Guinea: limited domesticable mammals, small-seeded native crops, geographic isolation. Introduced crops dramatically increase productivity when adopted. Agricultural capacity difference determined by species availability, not human capability or work ethic.

Historical & Geographic Context

Pre-contact New Guinea: productive agriculture within geographic constraints (taro, yams, sago). Post-contact: sweet potato adoption shows cultural flexibility and economic motivation. Modern New Guinea: tribes with access to introduced species (maize, rice, chickens, pigs) outcompeting traditional-agriculture tribes.
