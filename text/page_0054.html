Summary

Moriori's predictable defeat illustrates geographic determinism: Moriori were isolated hunter-gatherers with simple weapons, no war experience, peaceful traditions. Maori were dense-population farmers, chronically warring, advanced weapons, organized leadership. Despite Moriori numerical advantage (2:1), Maori slaughtered, enslaved Moriori population. Disparate development sealed military outcome despite common ancestry.

Key Concepts

Military outcome entirely predictable from technology/organization differences. Peaceful vs warlike tradition. Hunter-gatherer vs farmer subsistence. Organizational complexity determines warfare effectiveness. Divergent adaptation of Polynesian peoples produced military disparity despite <1000 years separation.

Historical & Geographic Context

1835 invasion: 900 Maori conquered Moriori. Moriori tradition: peaceful dispute resolution. Maori tradition: chronic warfare, organized armies. Centuries of geographic isolation created divergent cultural-military systems.

Connection to Main Thesis

Moriori-Maori collision exemplifies geographic determinism on small scale. Environmental constraints (Chatham cold climate) forced agricultural abandonment, reversed Moriori to hunter-gatherers. New Zealand warm climate maintained agriculture, supporting warrior development. Geographic environment determined military capacity within same founding population.
