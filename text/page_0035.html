Summary

Diamond establishes 11,000 BCE as the "starting line" for comparing continental development. This date marks the Ice Age's end, beginning of village life and domestication, and Americas' first undisputed human settlement. At this point, had some continents gained advantages over others?

Key Concepts

The chapter's premise: if head starts existed in 11,000 BCE, 13,000 years of amplification might explain global inequality by 1532 CE. The "starting line" metaphor suggests fair competition thereafter, with geography determining winners/losers from inception.

Historical & Geographic Context

11,000 BCE marks the Holocene epoch's beginning, post-Pleistocene climate stabilization, and independently-emerged food production (Fertile Crescent). All continents possessed humans by this date, allowing comparative analysis of development trajectories.

Connection to Main Thesis

This page establishes that geography's influence on human development must be assessed from 11,000 BCE onward. By showing all humans faced identical conditions then, any subsequent divergence stems from geographical differences, not innate population differences.
