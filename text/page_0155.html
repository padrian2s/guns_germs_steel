Summary

Fallacy: Aboriginal Australians entering modern world as Stone Age hunter-gatherers assumed "primitive forever." Hypothetical: alien visitor observing Earth 3000 BCE would see no food production in eastern United States (agriculture began ~2500 BCE). Would incorrectly conclude eastern US would forever remain hunter-gatherer. Temporal snapshot mistaken for permanent condition.

Key Concepts

Temporal snapshots vs. permanent conditions. Geographic constraints may delay adoption without preventing it. Technology diffusion timescale varies by geography/distance/barriers. Early contact ethnography documented pre-agricultural stage but falsely implied permanence. Development trajectories geography-determined but not unchangeable.

Historical & Geographic Context

Aboriginal Australians: 65,000 years hunter-gatherers pre-contact. Eastern US: hunter-gatherers 10,000+ BCE, adopted agriculture ~2500 BCE, developed after diffusion. Didn't stay hunter-gatherers permanently; geographic barriers delayed adoption. Contact ethnography documented pre-agricultural stage but incorrectly implied inherent permanence.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic barriers delay development without preventing it. Aboriginal Australia's isolation preserved pre-agricultural economy. Eastern US adopted agriculture upon exposure. Geography determines timing not destiny. Regions lacking domesticable species remain hunter-gatherers permanently. Geographic isolation can preserve pre-agricultural economies indefinitely. Domesticable species availability determines trajectory inevitability.
