Summary

Agriculture paradox: Farmers often worse off than hunter-gatherers. Time budget studies: farmers work as many or more hours than hunters. Archaeological evidence: first farmers shorter, less healthy, suffered more disease, lower life expectancy than preceding hunters. Yet agriculture spread - why adopt inferior system? Answer: population growth pressure forced adoption despite quality-of-life decline.

Key Concepts

Agriculture as necessity, not preference. Agriculture supports higher population density despite lower individual health/leisure. Populations forced to agriculture by numbers exceeding wild food carrying capacity. Agriculture spreads not because superior for individuals but because necessary for population survival at higher densities.

Historical & Geographic Context

Post-Ice Age ~11000 BP: megafauna extinction, wild food decline. Population growth continued. Regions unable to support hunter-gatherer density adopted agriculture despite health costs. Archaeological record shows first farmers malnourished, disease-prone compared to hunters. Population pressure forced transition despite individual disadvantage.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic wild food carrying capacity determines adoption necessity. Regions with abundant game/wild plants supported hunter-gatherers despite population growth.
