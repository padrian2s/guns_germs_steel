Summary

Disease transmission mechanisms: passive carriage strategies maximize spread. Insect vectors (mosquitoes, fleas, lice, tsetse): spread malaria, plague, typhus, sleeping sickness. Maternal transmission (fetus): perpetuates syphilis. Multiple transmission routes demonstrate evolutionary adaptation. Geography determines insect vector availability. Tropical vs temperate vectors: climate determines disease transmission feasibility.

Key Concepts

Vector transmission varies by geography. Tropical regions: rich insect fauna enables diverse transmission. Temperate regions: fewer vectors but distinct disease ecology. Climate determines vector species distribution. Disease transmission pathways geography-determined. Maternal transmission: fertility patterns determine syphilis persistence. Pregnancy common social constant: transmission guaranteed.

Historical & Geographic Context

Malaria: mosquito-transmitted tropical disease (geography-bound). Plague: flea-transmitted in rodent-dense areas (geography-bound). Sleeping sickness: tsetse fly African-endemic. Syphilis: maternal transmission introduced in Americas post-Columbus. Each disease transmission method reflects geographic ecology. Old World insect faunas evolved disease transmission relationships. Americas insects lacked evolved disease ecology partnerships.
