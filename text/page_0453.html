Summary

Bibliography on Indo-European origins and language distribution: Renfrew (Archaeology and Language) and Mallory (In Search of Indo-Europeans) examining controversial Indo-European origin questions. Russian Siberian expansion sources: Lantzeff/Pierce (Eastward to Empire) and Lincoln (Conquest of a Continent). Comprehensive documentation of geographic population expansion and language dispersal patterns.

Key Concepts

Indo-European expansion: geographic dispersal of language family from Central Asian origin. Language distribution reflects population movement patterns. Russian Siberian conquest: geographic expansion across vast territories. Colonial expansion: population movement enabled by technology advantages. Geographic expansion enables language diffusion: new populations speak expanding language replacing previous language families.

Historical & Geographic Context

Indo-European origins ~4000 BCE Central Asia. Expansion eastward to India, westward to Europe, southward to Middle East. Geographic horse domestication advantage enabled cavalry-based expansion. Russian Siberia conquest: 16th-17th century geographic expansion using firearms advantage. Geographic expansion patterns: mounted warriors (Indo-European), firearms (Russians) enable geographic conquest enabling language replacement.

Connection to Main Thesis

Language distribution reflects geographic-enabled expansion patterns. Geographic technology advantages (horses, firearms) enable population expansion enabling language replacement. Indo-European expansion geography-determined: Central Asian geography enabled horse domestication enabling cavalry enabling expansion enabling language spread. Russian Siberian expansion: firearms advantage enabled geographic conquest enabling Russian language expansion. Language family distribution documents geographic-determined population movements. Population expansion success reflects geographic resource availability enabling technology development enabling conquest.
