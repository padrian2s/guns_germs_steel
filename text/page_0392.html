Summary

Modern Africa's four native language families (Nilo-Saharan, Niger-Congo, Afroasiatic, Khoisan) survived not through intrinsic linguistic superiority but historical accident. Ancestral speakers "happened to be living at the right place and time." Geographic advantages enabled expansion enabled language family dominance. Language survival geography-determined, not language-quality-determined.

Key Concepts

Language survival geography-dependent not quality-dependent. Speakers with geographic advantages expand displace other speakers. Ancestral language family advantage determined by homeland geography not linguistic structure. Right place + right time = geographic advantages enabling expansion. Language families surviving because speakers had geographic advantages (domesticable animals/crops) enabling expansion.

Historical & Geographic Context

Afroasiatic: Mediterranean origin, domestication advantage enabled expansion North Africa Middle East. Niger-Congo: Bantu branch expanded agriculture advantage from Cameroon/Nigeria. Nilo-Saharan: fragmented distribution shows expansion limitations (geographic barriers, competing expansions). Khoisan: marginal survival reflects displacement by Bantu. Austronesian Madagascar: arrived via Indian Ocean trade routes. Survived four families those with geographic expansion advantages.

Connection to Main Thesis

Language family dominance reflects geographic advantage not linguistic superiority. Surviving language families (Afroasiatic, Niger-Congo, Nilo-Saharan) maintained by geographic advantage-enabled population expansion.
