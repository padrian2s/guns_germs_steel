Key Concepts

Language distribution as geographic evidence of population replacement. Fragmented enclaves indicate indigenous population remnants surrounded by conquerors. Geographic displacement patterns traceable through linguistic distribution. Large consolidated territories indicate successful expansion; fragmented small enclaves indicate resistance/survival of pre-expansion populations. Language geography reveals population movement and conquest patterns.

Historical & Geographic Context

Miao-Yao enclaves in South China: pre-Han populations surviving conquest as language islands. Han Chinese language/culture expansion from north gradually replacing indigenous southern populations. Geographic fragmentation shows incomplete replacementâ€”indigenous Miao-Yao surviving in scattered refuges. Contrast: Han consolidated territory across most of China. Language distribution maps population conquest, expansion, and replacement geography. Geographic survival of minority languages indicates regional resistance to Han consolidation.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic expansion patterns determine linguistic/cultural distribution. Han expansion from resource-rich northern regions gradually replacing southern populations. Miao-Yao linguistic fragmentation geographic evidence of incomplete conquest. Geography influenced expansion routes and resistance capacity. Mountain/difficult terrain enabled minority language survival (Miao-Yao enclaves in South China hills). Relatively accessible plains enabled Han consolidation. Geographic terrain determined conquest effectiveness and population replacement patterns.
