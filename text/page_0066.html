Summary

Polynesian societies ranged from simple egalitarian villages (Chathams) to stratified empires (Hawaii). Chiefs' powers and ranks increased with population density and island size. Hawaii developed eight hierarchically-ranked chiefly lineages with commoners forbidden intermarriage. Chiefs controlled land, conscripted labor, collected tributes. Social complexity directly proportional to geographic carrying capacity and population size generated by geography.

Key Concepts

Metallurgy technology geography-dependent on metal ore availability. Eurasia: iron abundant, iron-working technology. Americas: precious metals in South America, iron absent. Polynesia: no metal deposits except New Zealand. Metallurgy development impossible without geographic metal availability. Technology development constrained by geographic resource presence. Metal absence determines maximum technology achievement possible: stone-working technology ceiling.
