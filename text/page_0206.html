Summary

Bubonic plague first appeared Europe 542-543 CE (Plague of Justinian). Black Death epidemics 1346 CE required new trade route. Eurasian east-west axis: flea-infested furs from Central Asia plague areas to Europe rapidly. Geographic trade routes determine disease spread speed. Modern jet planes enable even faster intercontinental disease spread. Technology accelerates plague transmission.

Key Concepts

Trade route geography determines disease diffusion. East-west continental axis enables rapid diffusion (Eurasian silk road). Population contact routes determine epidemic transmission. Technology accelerates transmission (silk road â†’ jets). Geographic isolation prevents disease arrival. Eurasia interconnection enables disease cascade.

Historical & Geographic Context

Plague of Justinian 542-543: early European appearance via Mediterranean trade. Black Death 1346: new overland China-Europe route opened rapid transmission. Fur trade: reservoir species (rodents) transported with merchandise. Eurasian east-west orientation: enables trade connectivity. Americas north-south orientation: limited north-south trade (climate barriers). European-Asian trade: plague pathway. Americas isolation: no trans-continental plague trade.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic axis orientation determines trade pattern feasibility. East-west Eurasian axis: supports trade. East-west trade: supports disease diffusion. Americas north-south axis: limited trade (climate barriers). Limited trade: limited disease diffusion. Plague spread determined by Eurasian geographic configuration. Americas protected by geography from Eurasian plague. Technology now accelerates plague spread but geography initially determined who experienced plagues.
