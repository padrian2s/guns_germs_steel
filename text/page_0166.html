Summary

Big mammal domestication timeline ended ~2500 BCE with camels. No significant large herbivore domestications since thenâ€”5000+ years. Small mammals domesticated later (rabbits in Middle Ages, mice/rats 20th century labs, hamsters 20th century pets) but insignificant agriculturally. Large domesticable mammal pool exhausted by ancient times. Genetic limits prevent future big mammal domestication.

Key Concepts

Domestication exhaustion. Only 14 large mammals domesticated globally. No additions for 5000 years despite intensive human effort. Genetic constraints absolute: species lacking domestication traits cannot be domesticated regardless of human motivation. Small mammal domestication unrelated to agriculture (pets, laboratory). Agricultural societies' domesticate inventory frozen since Neolithic.

Historical & Geographic Context

Big mammal domestication: sheep/goat ~10000 BCE, pig ~9000 BCE, horse ~4000 BCE, camel ~2500 BCE. Post-2500 BCE: zero major domesticates added. Meanwhile, small mammals domesticated much later: rabbits ~1000 CE, mice/rats ~1900 CE, hamsters ~1950 CE. Agricultural societies never found additional large herbivores capable of domestication despite centuries effort.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic domesticable fauna inventory finite and unchanging. 14 species represents complete domestication capacity globally. No new large mammals domesticated in 5000 years proves genetic exhaustion. Present-day domesticate inventory reflects ancient geography's fauna limits. Regions with more large herbivores (Eurasia) achieved higher domestication capacity. Geographic fauna distribution determined Neolithic domestication; that Neolithic inventory persists unchanged.
