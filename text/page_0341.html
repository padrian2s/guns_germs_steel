Summary

Austronesian expansion map and chronology: wave-like expansion from Taiwan origin (4,000 years ago) across Southeast Asia to Pacific islands. Borneo/Celebes/Timor: 2500 B.C. (4,500 years ago). Java/Sumatra: 2000 B.C. (4,000 years ago). Bismarck Archipelago/Solomons: 1600 B.C. (3,600 years ago). Polynesia (Tonga, New Caledonia): 1200 B.C. (3,200 years ago). Society Islands: 1000 B.C. (3,000 years ago). Rapid geographic expansion across vast distances demonstrating successful maritime navigation and settlement capability.

Key Concepts

Expansion timeline documents geographic spread rate: 4,500 years for near regions, 3,000 years for distant Pacific. Maritime technology enabled unprecedented geographic expansion speed. Ocean geography enables communication between isolated islands enabling coordinated expansion. Expansion waves successive settlement replacing indigenous populations. Distance and time correlation: farther distances require more time enabling settlement processes.

Historical & Geographic Context

Taiwan origin 4,000 years ago: agricultural population with maritime technology. Near islands Indonesia 2500-2000 B.C.: nearby accessible regions settled first. Melanesia 1600-1200 B.C.: far regions requiring longer navigation. Polynesia 1200-1000 B.C.: remotest islands settled last. Geographic distance determined colonization timing: 500 year delay between Indonesia and Melanesia, additional 300-400 year delay for distant Polynesia. Systematic outward expansion following maritime route accessibility.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic distances determine population expansion timing and rate. Maritime technology enables geographic expansion enabling island colonization. Technology sophistication (ocean-going boats) determines distance capability determining expansion extent. Geographic distances create temporal delays in colonization: far regions colonized centuries after nearby regions. Chronology documents geographic determinism: technology capability limits distance limits expansion rate. Geographic opportunity (island chains) enables sequential expansion along island routes.
