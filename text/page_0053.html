Summary

Chapter 2 opens with Moriori-Maori collision (1835): Maori invaded Chatham Islands with 900 warriors, enslaved and killed Moriori population. Outcome easily predictable: Moriori were hunter-gatherers with simple technology, peaceful traditions, no war experience; Maori were farmers, chronically warlike, advanced weapons, organized leadership. Disparate development of two Polynesian peoples provided template for understanding broader continental differences.

Key Concepts

Moriori-Maori collision: small-scale natural experiment testing environmental effects on societies. Both groups Polynesian, diverged <1000 years prior. Historical & Geographic Context

Moriori settlement Chatham Islands after Maori colonized New Zealand ~1000 CE. Centuries of separation. 1835: Australian seal-ship brought news of Chathams to New Zealand, triggering Maori invasion. Direct cause: geographic isolation creating knowledge lag.

Connection to Main Thesis

Chapter 2 establishes smaller-scale natural experiment before examining continents. Same founding population diverged dramatically based on environmental constraints. Demonstrates geography's powerful influence on technology, politics, warfare capacity within short timeframe. Sets up detailed Polynesian analysis showing environmental factors driving societal complexity.
