Summary

Why New Guinea groups remain nomadic bands vs. settled large groups elsewhere: geographic resource constraints. Region lacks dense local resource concentrations permitting many people to live together. Lacked native plants enabling productive farming (until recent missionary crop introductions). Food staple: sago palm. Nomadic band settlement pattern reflects environmental carrying capacity limits.

Key Concepts

Population carrying capacity geography-determined. Nomadism reflects optimal strategy given resource distribution. Dense resources enable sedentism; dispersed resources enforce nomadism. Agricultural domestication requires domesticable plants; lack of suitable crops forces continued foraging. Geographic plant availability determines whether sedentary farming societies possible. Nomadic organization efficient adaptation to scattered resource environments, not inferior cultural choice.

Historical & Geographic Context

New Guinea swamp geography: limited resource concentration, sago palm as sole staple. Contrast: river valleys (Egypt, Mesopotamia, China) supported dense populations through concentrated agricultural resources. Pacific islands varied: islands with dense tropical resources (taro, coconut) developed large settlements; resource-poor islands remained sparsely settled. Temperate regions with cereal domestication enabled massive population expansion. Connection to Main Thesis

Political organization follows geographic carrying capacity. Nomadic bands appropriate settlement pattern for resource-sparse environments. Sedentary states require geographic conditions enabling agriculture surplus. Geographic domesticable plant availability determines sedentism possibility. Geographic resource density determines optimal political organization. Population settlement patterns and political structure directly follow from geography's resource distribution patterns, not from human differences.
