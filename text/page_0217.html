Summary

Alphabet writing system: unique signs (letters) represent basic language sounds (phonemes). Most alphabets: ~20-30 letters only. Most languages: more phonemes than alphabet letters. English: ~40 phonemes with 26 letters only. Alphabets force multiple letters representing single sounds. Alphabetic writing efficiency trade-offs. Writing system development reflects language structure.

Key Concepts

Alphabet efficiency: fewer symbols represent more sounds. Writing system efficiency determines adoption feasibility. Alphabetic systems more learnable than complex syllabaries. Simpler writing systems enable greater literacy spread. Literacy spread enables larger literate populations. Larger literate populations enable larger organizations.

Historical & Geographic Context

Phoenician alphabet: 22 letters enabling efficient writing. Alphabet adoption spread through Mediterranean civilizations. Greek, Latin alphabets derived from Phoenician. Alphabetic efficiency enabled wide adoption. Complex writing systems (Chinese ~3000+ characters): limited literacy. Alphabetic simplicity: enabled mass literacy when societies reached complexity level.

Connection to Main Thesis

Alphabet efficiency determines writing system adoption rate. Writing adoption rate determines literacy spread. Literacy spread determines organizational scale. Americas limited-alphabet writing systems (Mayan glyphic system more complex): lower adoption/literacy rates. European alphabetic writing: higher literacy enabling larger organizations.
