Few Eurasian hunter-gatherers remained: Ainu northern Japan, Siberian groups without reindeer, scattered forest enclaves (India, SE Asia) trading with farmers. Key Concepts

Agriculture geographic-determined through plant availability. Eurasia plant diversity enabled agriculture adoption across most regions. Marginal regions (Arctic, mountains) unsuitable agriculture retained hunting-gathering. Pastoralism alternative to agriculture in marginal regions. Agricultural adoption near-universal when geography permits. Geography determines agricultural versus pastoral strategies.

Historical & Geographic Context

Eurasia 1492: agriculture dominant across most regions. Temperate zones: farming universal. Desert/steppe regions: pastoralism. Arctic: hunting-gathering (harsh climate prevents both agriculture and herding). Mountains: hunting-gathering or pastoral (elevation unsuitable farming). Geography determined subsistence strategy distribution. Reindeer herding Siberia specialized adaptation to extreme climate. Scattered forest hunting (India/SE Asia) marginal refuge populations.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic climate/plant availability determines subsistence strategy determines population distribution. Agriculture possible most regions except marginal climate/elevation. Pastoralism alternative marginal regions unsuitable agriculture. Agriculture/pastoralism specialization geography-determined through environmental suitability. Hunting-gathering remaining only where both agriculture and pastoralism impossible (Arctic). Geographic environment determines economic organization determines political complexity determines civilization level.
