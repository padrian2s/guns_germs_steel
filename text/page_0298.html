Summary

New Guinea-Australia contrast: New Guinea farmers/swineherds, settled villages, tribal organization, bows/arrows, pottery. Substantial dwellings, seaworthy boats, varied utensils. Higher population density than Australians due to food production. Australia: hunter-gatherers, nomadic bands, no agriculture, lower population density. Geographic resource availability determines subsistence mode determines population density determines societal complexity.

Key Concepts

Food production geography-determined through domesticable species availability. Agriculture enables settled villages, higher density, tribal organization. Hunting/gathering enables only bands, low density. Technology complexity (pottery, boats, tools) enables higher living standards. Population density enables political complexity (tribes vs. bands). Geography determines resource availability determines subsistence determines density determines organization.

Historical & Geographic Context

New Guinea: tropical crops available (taro, yams, sago), pigs available for domestication. Food production viable. Agricultural surplus supported settled populations, crafts development (pottery, boats). Australia: minimal domesticable plants/animals, hunting-gathering only viable subsistence. No food surplus, no population concentration, no craft specialization, no tribal organization development.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic domesticable species availability determines subsistence mode determines population density determines societal organization. New Guinea geography enabled agriculture enabling complexity. Australia geography prevented agriculture forcing hunter-gatherer adaptation. Difference in resources created difference in development not human capability difference. Same people in opposite geographies would show opposite development patterns.
