Summary

New Guinea altitude determines agriculture viability: mid-montane zone (4,000-9,000 feet) only suitable for intensive agriculture. No food production above 9,000 feet (alpine), little production 1,000-4,000 feet, only low-density slash-and-burn below 1,000 feet. Geographic terrain fragmentation limits population concentration. Limited food surplus limits craft specialization, professional classes. Fragmented geography prevents large-scale economic exchanges between altitude zones.

Key Concepts

Elevation determines crop suitability. Montane agriculture viability narrow elevation band. Geographic segmentation by altitude prevents large populations. No surplus generation in unsuitable zones. Fragmented altitude zones prevent trade/specialization supporting complex civilization. Geography determines settlement possibilities through elevation climate suitability.

Historical & Geographic Context

New Guinea mountains: multiple altitude zones with different crops/productivity. Mid-montane agricultural zone highest productivity. Alpine above 9,000 feet: too cold for crops. Lowlands 0-1,000 feet: disease environment discourages settlement, limits crops. Only narrow montane band permits intensive agriculture. Geographic segmentation isolated communities preventing regional states.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic terrain determines agriculture distribution determining population distribution determining political organization. Montane restriction on agriculture limits total population preventing state-scale development. Elevation fragmentation prevents regional consolidation. Geography through terrain determines whether population density sufficient for state formation.
