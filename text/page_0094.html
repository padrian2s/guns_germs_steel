Summary

Counterintuitive finding: earliest agricultural sites (Iraq, Iran, Mexico, Andes, parts of China) were dry or ecologically degraded regions, not modern breadbaskets. Later, agriculture spread to modern breadbaskets (US, England, France, Indonesia, sub-equatorial Africa) which were still hunter-gatherer in prehistory. Agriculture arose in marginal lands first, spread secondarily.

Key Concepts

Agricultural diffusion and timing. Agriculture not inevitable globally, emerges where suitable wild species available (regardless of current environmental quality). Diffusion delayed agricultural adoption in high-potential zones. Early adopters achieved critical advantages long before agriculture reached more environmentally favorable regions.

Historical & Geographic Context

Fertile Crescent: dry climate, early domestication ~10000 BCE. Mesoamerica: marginal maize origins, ~5000 BCE. China: millet/rice in suboptimal zones. European temperate zones: agriculture arrived 5000-3000 BCE from diffusion, not independent invention. American Great Plains: agriculture limited by continental latitude barriers.

Connection to Main Thesis

Timing matters: regions adopting agriculture first developed civilizational advantages before agriculture reached neighboring regions. Environmental quality irrelevant if lacking domesticable species. Agricultural spread limited by geography (continental axes, climate barriers). Early-adopting regions perpetuated advantage by 1492. Geographic location (not resource richness) determined power differential at contact.
