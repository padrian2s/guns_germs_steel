Summary

Anna Karenina Principle applied to animal domestication: domesticable animals require many specific traits; lack of any single required trait dooms domestication (like unhappy marriages, each failure unique). Chapter identifies six groups of reasons for failed domestication. Diet constraints first example: animals with unsuitable dietary needs cannot be domesticated regardless of human effort.

Key Concepts

Domestication requirements multiple: diet, temperament, size, growth rate, breeding patterns, social structure. Zebra example: unsuitable temperament despite otherwise suitable traits (size, diet). Each unsuitable species fails for different genetic reason. Multiple constraints necessary simultaneously; single failure prevents domestication entirely.

Historical & Geographic Context

Domestication requirements: herbivores preferred (graze on cheap vegetation), peaceful temperament, hierarchical social structure, rapid growth, high reproductive rates, calm response to captivity. Horses meet all requirements. Zebras lack temperament (biting propensity). Cheetahs cannot breed in captivity. Each failure species exemplifies different constraint.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic fauna behavioral genetics determine domestication feasibility. Eurasian animals possess multiple domestication traits. Americas/African fauna lack specific critical traits. Geography-distributed fauna behavioral genetics determine which animals domesticable where. Six domestication requirement categories geography-distributed across global fauna. Single missing trait in any animal prevents domestication entirely.
