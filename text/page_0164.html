Summary

African examples of domesticated animal impact. Khoisan peoples acquiring cattle/sheep ~2000 years ago displaced hunter-gatherer populations across southern Africa. Domestic horse arrival in West Africa ~1000 years ago transformed warfare, enabled cavalry kingdoms. Trypanosome disease (tsetse fly) limited horse expansion beyond West Africa. Animal technology geography-constrained by disease.

Key Concepts

Animal domestication transformative power. Pastoral adoption: pastoral societies displace hunter-gatherers. Cavalry advantage: horses enable military dominance, state formation. Geographic disease constraints: tsetse fly limits horse distribution. Domesticated animals provide advantage; disease ecology limits geographic expansion.

Historical & Geographic Context

Southern Africa: Khoisan adoption of cattle/sheep ~2000 years ago (from Bantu diffusion from north). Pastoral societies displaced forager populations. West Africa: horse adoption transformed region into cavalry-dependent kingdoms (Mali, Songhai empires). Sahel geography enabled horse use. Tsetse fly disease belt prevented horse penetration into sub-Saharan Africa, preserving forager societies.

Connection to Main Thesis

Domesticated animals geography-dependent through disease ecology. Horses transform societies where available but disease limits geographic range. Tsetse fly geography-distributed, constrains horse expansion. Same domesticated animals available in some African regions but not others based on disease ecology. Geography's disease patterns determine which animal technologies applicable where.
