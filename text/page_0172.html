Summary

Zebra temperament prevents domestication despite physical suitability. Zebras bite and retain grip (injure zookeepers more than tigers annually). Impossible to lasso despite similar size to horses. Zebras watch rope and dodge skillfully. Temperament genetics prevent domestication despite human effort and motivation. Size alone insufficient without behavioral traits.

Key Concepts

Temperament genetics absolute constraint. Physical similarity (zebra vs. horse) irrelevant without behavioral compatibility. Horses domesticable; zebras permanently wild. Genetic aggression/intelligence traits prevent domestication. Human inability to overcome genetic constraints proves geographic fauna genetics determine domestication possibility.

Historical & Geographic Context

Horses: domesticated across Eurasia despite wild ancestors. Zebras: never domesticated despite African availability and human effort. Temperament difference: horses accept human hierarchy, zebras resist violently. Biting behavior, evasion intelligence, aggressive temperament genetic traits preventing domestication.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic fauna behavioral genetics determine domestication outcome. African zebras genetically unsuitable for domestication despite African presence. Eurasian horses genetically suitable. Geographic fauna distribution + genetic traits = domestication feasibility. Zebras prove that geographic location plus size insufficient; genetic temperament required.
