Summary

Domesticated animals provided agricultural productivity advantage: plow-capable animals (oxen, cattle, yak) enabled cultivation of heavy soils. Linearbandkeramik culture (5000 BCE) confined to hand-dig soils; ox-drawn plows arrived 1000+ years later, expanding cultivation exponentially to new agricultural zones.

Key Concepts

Animal-powered agriculture. Plows drawn by animals dramatically increase arable land. Technology adoption depends on available domesticated animals. Societies with horses/oxen/yaks could expand rapidly; societies without suitable animals remained limited to manual cultivation or pastoralism.

Historical & Geographic Context

Neolithic Europe: Light-soil agriculture ~5000 BCE, manual labor. Plow adoption 4000-3000 BCE opened heavy soil agriculture in Central Europe (Danube basin). Eurasian steppes: horse domestication enabled vast territory exploitation. Americas: llamas/alpacas insufficient for plow-agriculture development.

Connection to Main Thesis

Available domesticable animals determine agricultural technology trajectory. Eurasian fauna (horses, oxen, yak) enabled plow technology, expanding arable land dramatically. Americas lacked such animals, limiting agricultural expansion to small-scale manual cultivation. Geographic fauna variation cascades into agricultural productivity divergence.
