Summary

Why agriculture over hunting-gathering? Agricultural calories exceed wild food productivity. But agriculture depends on suitable wild species for domestication. Only small minority of wild species are: edible to humans, worth hunting, edible when processed, possible to gather efficiently, safe to approach.

Key Concepts

Wild species limitations. Most species unsuitable for human food: indigestible (bark), poisonous (monarch butterflies, mushrooms), low nutrition (jellyfish), tedious to prepare (tiny nuts), hard to gather (insect larvae), dangerous to hunt (rhinoceros). Geography determines which usable species available.

Historical & Geographic Context

Agricultural transition requires species availability. Fertile Crescent had wild wheat, barley, lentils, sheep, goats (domesticable). Americas had maize, beans, squash (adequate) but no large domesticable mammals. Africa and Australia: limited domesticable crops. Species availability geographic constraint.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic variation in species determines agricultural feasibility. Regions with naturally occurring large-seeded grasses and domesticable animals transition to farming. Regions lacking such species remain hunter-gatherers or adopt agriculture much later. This determines population trajectory and civilizational capacity.
