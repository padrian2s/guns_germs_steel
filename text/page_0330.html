Summary

Chinese agricultural diversity: ducks, geese, pigs domesticated regionally. Crops: soybeans, hemp, citrus, tea, apricots, peaches, pears. Eurasia east-west axis permitted crop/animal spread: Chinese domesticates spread westward; West Asian domesticates spread eastward. Wheat, barley, cattle, horses from West Asia became significant in ancient China. Bidirectional diffusion across continents.

Key Concepts

Eurasian axis enables crop-animal diffusion. East-west alignment permits climate-similar diffusion. Bidirectional technology spread. West Asia-China interaction enriched both civilizations. Geographic connectivity determines innovation diffusion. Continental shape determines diffusion feasibility. Latitude-aligned continents enable rapid technology spread.

Historical & Geographic Context

Silk Road: ancient trade facilitating crop/animal diffusion between China and West Asia. Horses transformed Chinese warfare capacity. Wheats/barleys improved Chinese agricultural productivity. Chinese crops (soybeans, tea) spread westward. Americas lacked equivalent north-south accessibility limiting north-south diffusion. African diffusion similarly challenged by desert barriers.

Connection to Main Thesis

Continental axis geography determines diffusion patterns. Eurasia east-west orientation enables rapid technology spread. Americas north-south orientation limits diffusion: climate variation prevents easy spread. Africa north-south similar constraints. Geographic continent shape directly determines which innovations spread widely vs. regionally concentrated. Diffusion patterns geography-fundamentally determined.
