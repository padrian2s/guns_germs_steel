Summary

Aboriginal Australians: sophisticated landscape management without agriculture. Burning fields to encourage edible seed plants. Yam cultivation including replanting for regeneration. Mimicked agriculture mechanics without full adoption. Lack of suitable domesticable plants prevented transition despite environmental management sophistication.

Key Concepts

Agricultural practices without agriculture domestication. Proto-agricultural techniques insufficient without domesticable species. Environment management alone cannot substitute for lacking wild plants. Geographic species constraints override cultural capability or management sophistication.

Historical & Geographic Context

Aboriginal Australia: landscape burning, yam tending, seed plant management for millennia. Never transitioned to agriculture despite proto-agricultural practices. Lack of large grain crops, herbivorous animals prevented agricultural development. Geographic isolation and species limitations determined outcome.

Connection to Main Thesis

Aboriginal management sophistication irrelevant without domesticable species. Proves geography, not capability, determines agriculture. Australia's isolation prevented species diffusion. Australians could manage introduced crops successfully post-contact. Lack of agriculture pre-contact purely geographic, not cultural limitation.
