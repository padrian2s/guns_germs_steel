Summary

Major epidemic diseases: human-exclusive, evolutionary survivors. Most pathogens attempting human jump from animals fail. Few zoonotic agents successfully establish human transmission. Disease evolution stages: animal origin â†’ intermediate stage (infects both) â†’ human exclusive. Vector changes: pathogens evolve new transmission mechanisms adapted to humans.

Key Concepts

Zoonotic emergence: animal-to-human disease jumping. Most jump failures: species barriers prevent transmission establishment. Successful jumps: rare pathogens find viable human transmission. Vector evolution: adapting intermediate vectors. Host specialization: pathogens evolve human-specific mechanisms. Natural selection filters: unsuccessful pathogens eliminated, successful ones persist.

Historical & Geographic Context

Measles: bovine rinderpest origin, jumped ~2,500 years ago. Influenza: animal bird/swine origins, annual human recombination. HIV: simian origin, zoonotic jump 20th century. Each requires geographic contact: humans living near/eating animals. Domestication proximity: enables zoonotic jumping. Butchering practices: fecal contact enables transmission. Geography of animal contact determines zoonotic exposure.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic domesticable animals determine zoonotic reservoir availability. Animal domestication determines human-animal contact intensity. Contact determines zoonotic jumping probability. Successful jumps determined by population density (requires viable human transmission). Eurasian fauna: enabled multiple successful zoonotic jumps. Americas fauna: fewer domesticated animals, fewer successful zoonotic jumps. Geography determines fauna determines animals determines zoonotic exposure determines epidemic disease availability.
