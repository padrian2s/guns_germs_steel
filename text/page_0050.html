Summary

By ~11,000 BCE, world's most habitable continents supported humans. Island settlement completed in modern times (Mediterranean, Caribbean, Polynesian, Madagascar, Iceland). An 11,000 BCE archaeologist, seeing continents' different settlement dates, must assess whether "head starts" mattered. Africa's 5+ million years protohuman advantage (vs other continents) and modern human origin there (~100,000 years ago) suggests African superiority.

Key Concepts

Head start concept problematic. Populating continent faster than assumed (e.g., <1000 years for Americas). Adaptation time varies (9,000 years for High Arctic). Early occupation of Eurasia long before other continents. But does "head start" matter in footrace metaphor?

Historical & Geographic Context

11,000 BCE world: all continents inhabited. Settlement dates: Africa earliest; Eurasia long-occupied; Americas, Australia, Polynesia more recent. Different occupation lengths set stage for comparing development rates.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic advantage isn't automatic from early settlement. Africa's head start meant nothing if environmental factors (domesticable species, climate) weren't present. True geographic advantage measured by resource availability, not time occupied. Sets up detailed analysis needed for each continent.
