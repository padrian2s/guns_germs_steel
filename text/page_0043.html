Summary

Human colonization of previously unpeopled islands resulted in megafauna extinction. The dodo (Mauritius), moas (New Zealand), and giant lemurs became extinct after human arrival. These extinctions followed a consistent pattern across all well-studied oceanic islands colonized prehistoricallyâ€”where protective measures occurred quickly, extinctions were prevented; where they didn't, extinction resulted.

Key Concepts

Megafauna extinction wave following human colonization. Extinction pattern consistent across diverse islands. Overkill hypothesis: humans directly killed extinct giants. Climate hypothesis as alternative explanation. Evidence: timing correlations with human arrival.

Historical & Geographic Context

Extinctions occurred on newly colonized islands: Mauritius (dodo), New Zealand (moas), Madagascar (giant lemurs). Timing varied by island colonization time. Each island colonization followed by megafauna extinction within centuries.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic isolation meant isolated megafauna with no predator experience. Human arrival with hunting technology devastated populations. Different continents and islands had different megafauna vulnerable to human huntingâ€”geography determined both fauna available and extinction vulnerability, affecting later domestication resources.
