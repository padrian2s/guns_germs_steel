Summary

Diseases biggest killers of people historically. War deaths pre-WWII: more from disease than battle wounds. Military history oversimplifies: disease, not general brilliance, determined war winners. Epidemiology determines military outcomes. Population disease immunity determines combat capability. Geographic disease exposure determines war winners independent of military strategy.

Key Concepts

Diseases as major technological advantage in conquest. Population immunity determines disease resistance in combat contact. Zoonotic disease exposure determines immune system development. Geographic fauna determines disease evolution patterns. Populations with disease-evolved immunity prevail in conquest. Disease epidemiology determines civilization expansion capability.

Historical & Geographic Context

Pre-WWII wars: disease casualties exceeded battle wounds. European conquest Americas: disease killed far more than weapons. Epidemiologic advantage: Europeans carried Old World diseases. Americas populations lacked immunity (no Old World domesticated fauna). Disease decimated native populations. Spanish conquest: disease not military superiority. Disease casualties demonstrate geographic determinism outcome.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic fauna determines zoonotic diseases available. Diseases determine population immunity evolution. Immunity determines military capability. Military capability determines conquest outcomes. Technology includes disease weaponization. Geographic fauna thus determines which populations can conquer others. Conquest patterns follow geographic disease distribution patterns not human brilliance. Geography determines war winners through epidemiology.
