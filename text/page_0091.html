Summary

Domesticated animals revolutionized land transport. Pre-animal: humans carried goods on backs, limiting quantity and distance. Domestic animals (horse, donkey, yak, camel) enabled heavy goods movement in large quantities over long distances rapidly, until railroad development in 19th century.

Key Concepts

Animal transport enables trade, military logistics, imperial administration. Horses allowed cavalry armies, rapid communication, supply lines over vast distances. Animal transport preceded mechanical transport by millennia, fundamental to empire-building capacity.

Historical & Geographic Context

Eurasian steppes: horse domestication enabled vast empire expansion (Mongols, Persians, Romans). Americas: llamas/alpacas, burden-capable only in Andes mountains at high altitudes. No continent-spanning animal transport possible in Americas, limiting military/administrative reach.

Connection to Main Thesis

Available domesticated animals determine transport infrastructure and imperial capacity. Horse-domesticating societies build continent-spanning empires. Animal-poor societies remain regionally fragmented. Geographic fauna distribution determines military logistics, trade networks, empire-building potential.
