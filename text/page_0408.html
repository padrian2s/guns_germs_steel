Summary

Four sets of geographic factors quantifiable and objectively measurable: domestication materials (mammal herbivores, cereals), environmental suitability, diffusion/migration rates, area + competing societies. Eurasia: largest landmass, most competing societies (enables competition driving innovation). Americas: large aggregate area but geographically/ecologically fragmented (functions as multiple smaller continents). Australia/Tasmania: smallest areas, fewest competing societies (limits innovation pressure). Geographic factors scientifically quantifiable enabling testable predictions.

Key Concepts

Geographic factors objective: continent size measurable, species available countable, competition intensity quantifiable. Scientific history: measurable variables predict outcomes. Large landmass enables competition drives innovation. Fragmented geography limits competition limits innovation. Area determines maximum population density determines maximum technology complexity. Competing societies drive technology advancement through competitive pressure.
