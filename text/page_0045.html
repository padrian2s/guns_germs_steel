Summary

The Americas were first colonized ~12,000 BCE (oldest unquestioned remains in Alaska). By 11,000 BCE, a profusion of Clovis culture sites appeared in the US and Mexico, characterized by distinctive large stone spearpoints. Hundreds of Clovis sites attest to rapid population expansion from Alaska to Patagonia.

Key Concepts

Clovis culture: standardized large stone spearpoints. Rapid population expansion south of Canadian border. 11,000-12,000 BCE timing. Clovis archaeological signature spreads across Americas quickly, suggesting single founding population expanding rapidly through available territory.

Historical & Geographic Context

Siberia colonization occurred by ~20,000 years ago. Bering land bridge crossing possible ~12,000-13,000 years ago when ice sheets receded. Alaska occupation ~12,000 BCE; rapid spread south before 11,000 BCE.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic barriers (Bering ice sheet) prevented earlier Americas colonization despite human presence in Siberia. Technology (cold-survival, watercraft) enabled crossing only after Great Leap Forward.
