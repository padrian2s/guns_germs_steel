Summary

Material culture variation: from simple personal utensils to monumental stone architecture. Available stone determined tools: atolls (no stone) forced clamshell adzes; volcanic islands (basalt available) enabled diverse stoneworking. New Zealand (continental fragment) accessed jade. Geographic geology directly determined tool types, craft complexity, monumental construction capacity. Raw material availability constrained cultural development.

Key Concepts

Raw material constraints determine technology development. Stone type availability (basalt, volcanic, granite) determines tool types. Geography without stone deposits (atolls) forces alternative materials (shell). Monumental construction impossible without sufficient stone/labor surplus. Geographic resource distribution determines craft specialization capacity.

Historical & Geographic Context

Polynesian islands demonstrate raw material variation: Chathams (no granite/flint, volcanic rocks only) produced simple tools; volcanic islands (basalt available) enabled diverse fishhooks/adzes/jewelry; New Zealand (continental fragment) accessed jade. Same culture, same starting technology, different raw materials produced completely divergent craft traditions.

Connection to Main Thesis

Polynesian material culture diversity proves geographic determinism through resource constraints. Identical populations/cultures produced divergent technologies through geology variation alone.
