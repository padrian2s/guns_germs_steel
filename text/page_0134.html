Summary

Failed domestications: similar species different domestication outcomes. Olives: wild species in Africa, southern Asia, eastern Australia never domesticated. Apples/grapes: Eurasian wild ancestors domesticated; North American wild relatives with same traits never domesticated. Modern hybridization uses wild American genetics to improve Eurasian crops.

Key Concepts

Domestication as geographic-cultural choice. Species suitability necessary but not sufficient. Cultural, economic, geographic factors determine adoption. Availability alone doesn't guarantee domestication. Time, location, population pressure influence domestication selectivity. Genetic similarity between domesticated and wild undocumented species.

Historical & Geographic Context

Olives: Eurasian domestication ~7000 BCE. Related African species available but never domesticated (diffusion barriers, wild olive preference, cultural factors). Apples: Eurasian domestication classical times. American wild apples available to indigenous peoples for millennia but never domesticated (no adoption pressure, diffusion barriers).

Connection to Main Thesis

Species availability insufficient for domestication. Geographic diffusion, wild productivity, cultural factors determine adoption. Americas' lack of apple/grape domestication not genetic barrier but geographic isolation preventing diffusion, wild food abundance reducing adoption pressure. Geographic location determines domestication adoption independent of genetic feasibility.
