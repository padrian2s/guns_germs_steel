Rezumat

Diamond examines alternative geographical explanations: the "irrigation hypothesis" suggests that large-scale irrigation in lowland river valleys (Tigris-Euphrates, Nile, Indus, Yellow/Yangtze, Maya lowlands, Peru's coast) required centralized bureaucracies, driving civilization development. However, archaeological evidence shows centralized bureaucracies arose first; complex irrigation systems followed. Early developments in these regions (food production, villages) occurred in hills/mountains, not lowland valleys.

Concepte Cheie

Diamond distinguishes cause from correlation. River valleys correlation with civilization reflects political centralization enabling irrigation, not irrigation causing civilization. The Nile Valley remained a cultural backwater for 3,000 years after Fertile Crescent civilization flourished in hills. Geography matters, but not in the way the irrigation hypothesis suggests.

Context Istoric È™i Geografic

The irrigation hypothesis gained archaeological credibility from Karl Wittfogel's "hydraulic civilization" theory (1950s), which attributed Oriental despotism to irrigation system management. Archaeological excavations have since contradicted the hypothesis, revealing civilization preceded irrigation in most regions.
