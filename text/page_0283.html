Key Concepts

Social contract theory: rational choice model assuming deliberate state adoption. Geographic determinism: state formation inevitable given geography enabling population density. Kleptocratic critique: populations wouldn't voluntarily accept exploitation. Geographic pressure model: sufficient population density creates organization pressure; states emerge necessarily not voluntarily. Regions unable to support state-scale populations remain tribal. Geography determines whether state formation possible, not whether populations choose states.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic determinism superior to social contract theory explaining state formation. Geography determines whether populations reach densities where states necessary. Population pressure (geography-driven) creates state emergence necessity. Geographic differences explain state distribution: high-agriculture regions developed states; low-agriculture regions remained tribal. State formation geography-inevitable, not rationally chosen.
