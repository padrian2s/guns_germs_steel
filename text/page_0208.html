Summary

Epidemic dies out when susceptible population exhausted (everyone infected or immune). O'nyong-nyong fever emerged East Africa 1959: infected millions. Likely monkey virus transmitted by mosquitoes. Geographic vector availability (mosquitoes) determines transmission feasibility. Patients recovered quickly: low mortality transmission favors establishment.

Key Concepts

Epidemic disease threshold: requires susceptible population. Disease burnout: when all susceptible infected or immune. Vector-borne diseases: geographic vector distribution determines transmission. Tropical mosquitoes: tropical disease ecology. Low-virulence zoonotic emergence: animal viruses jumping to humans. Geographic climate determines vector availability.

Historical & Geographic Context

O'nyong-nyong fever: tropical Africa mosquito vector disease. East Africa geographic location: mosquito-endemic region enabling rapid spread. Millions infected: sufficient population density for epidemic. Disease linked animal reservoirs (monkeys). Tropical climate vector ecology: geographic mosquito species distribution. Similar tropical arboviruses: yellow fever, dengue (geographic endemic regions).

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic climate determines vector species available. Vector species determine transmission mechanism. Geographic density determines epidemic sustainability.
