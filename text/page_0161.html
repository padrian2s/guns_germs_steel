Summary

Ancient Fourteen completion: yak (Himalayas), bali cattle (Southeast Asia), mithan (India/Burma). Aurochs relatives (banteng, gaur) regionally confined domestication. Limited distribution despite domestication success. Geographic domestication specificity. Dogs: universal domestication from wolf ancestors. Fourteen species represents complete domesticable mammal inventory.

Key Concepts

Regional domestication specialization. Mountain zones (yak), tropical zones (bali cattle), Indian subcontinent (mithan). Geographic constraint geography-determined. Dogs: success everywhere. Fourteen represents total domestication success globally. No additional species domesticated post-1500 CE despite increased contact/technology. Genetic constraints limiting domestication pool.

Historical & Geographic Context

Yak: Himalayan adaptation, limited expansion outside region. Bali cattle: Southeast Asian domestication, regional bound. Mithan: Indian subcontinent domestication, geographic constraint. Dogs: prehistoric universal domestication predating agriculture. Fourteen represents all domesticable large mammals identified across all continents across 10,000+ years.

Connection to Main Thesis

Regional specialization in remaining domestication reflects geographic availability. No new large mammal domestication since agriculture start indicates genetic exhaustion. Fourteen limit proves domesticable animal rarity. Geographic distribution proves uneven Eurasian dominance. Ancient Fourteen monopoly explains Eurasian technology advantage. Geographic animal distribution determines civilization capacity definitively.
