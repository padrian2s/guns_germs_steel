Summary

High population density regions (states and chiefdoms): defeated populations lack escape options. Victors exploit survivors two ways: slavery (chiefdoms/states with economic specialization) or tributary extraction (societies with intensive food production yielding large surpluses). Low-population-density regions: defeated bands flee to unclaimed lands, escape victors entirely. Population density determines exploitation feasibility. Dense populations forced integration into victor society; sparse populations escape through flight. Geography determining population density determines exploitation possibilities.

Historical & Geographic Context

Desert regions Australia: low density enabled defeated band escape to unoccupied lands. Fertile Crescent/Nile Egypt: high density, intensive agriculture prevented escape, enabled slavery systems. Mesoamerica: high-density Aztec empire maintained slave systems. Steppe regions: nomadic populations enabled escape despite conflicts. Temperate agricultural regions: high density enabled both slavery and tribute systems. Contrasts show consistent pattern: agriculture-enabled dense populations trapped defeated populations; sparse-population regions enabled escape.
