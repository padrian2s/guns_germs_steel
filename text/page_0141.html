Summary

Fertile Crescent biological diversity over small distances: key advantage. Farmers transplanted wild cereal seeds from rain-dependent hillsides to damp valley bottoms. Valley bottom irrigation ensured reliable growth independent of rainfall. Microhabitat diversity enabling cultivation optimization. Valleys provided agriculture insurance against climate variation.

Key Concepts

Microhabitat diversity advantage. Topographic variety enabling crop optimization. Water availability variation across small distances. Predictable water sources enabling confident cultivation. Hillside vs. valley exploitation creating agricultural security. Geographic microvariation enabling farmer flexibility in plant relocation decisions.

Historical & Geographic Context

Fertile Crescent: hills with rain-dependent wild cereals. Valleys with groundwater-fed damp bottoms. Farmers recognized transportation possibility. By ~9000 BCE: intentional seed translocation from hills to valleys. Early agriculture success partly relied on topographic diversity enabling cultivation experiment. Regions lacking topographic diversity limited to single elevation agricultural strategies.

Connection to Main Thesis

Topographic diversity determines agricultural experimentation capacity. Fertile Crescent valley-hill combination allowed rapid cultivation optimization. Flat regions lack elevation variation enabling different cultivation strategies. Microhabitat diversity geography-determined enables farmer innovation in crop selection/placement. Topographic variation geographic advantage for early agriculture development.
