Summary

Writing: European advantage spread conquest information rapidly. Columbus's voyages and Cortes's Mexico conquest reported in letters/pamphlets motivated Spanish emigration and provided sailing directions. Pizarro's captain printed detailed conquest report (Seville 1534, nine months after Atahuallpa's death). Writing technology enabled rapid information transmission, coordinated colonization effort. Americas lacked writing systems for similar communication advantage. Literacy gap enabled Europe to coordinate, learn, and amplify conquest momentum.

Key Concepts

Writing technology enables rapid information diffusion. Printed reports spread conquest success stories. Letters/pamphlets provided sailing directions enabling replication. Pizarro's companion report published nine months after Atahuallpa's death. Information speed transformed from oral communication to printed medium. Writing-printing technology accelerated colonization momentum. Americas lacked equivalent printing technology: information spread slower, coordination reduced.

Historical & Geographic Context

Columbus voyages reported (late 1400s), Cortes Mexico conquest reported (early 1500s), Pizarro conquest reported (1534 printed). Information transmission cascade motivated Spanish emigration to Americas. Detailed sailing directions published enabled navigation. Conquest success reports created economic incentive (treasure/wealth) driving colonization wave. Printing press technology (Gutenberg 1440s) enabled information diffusion speed. Americas lacked equivalent printing technology.
