Summary

African population diversity: Pygmies hunter-gatherers in Central African rainforest, trade/work with neighboring black farmers. Physical differences from blacks: smaller size, reddish skin (vs black), more body hair, different facial features. Geographic habitat determination: rainforest ecology supports hunting-gathering; surrounding savanna supports farming.

Key Concepts

Geographic habitat determines subsistence technology. Rainforest optimal for hunting-gathering: dense game, abundant plants. Savanna optimal for farming: grassland domesticable animals, crops. Different habitats enable different subsistence strategies. Pygmies + neighboring farmers: different technologies reflecting geographic habitat suitability not biological differences.

Historical & Geographic Context

Central African rainforest: game animals (forest buffalo, forest elephants), dense plant resources enabling hunting-gathering. Savanna surrounding: grassland animals (cattle) domesticable, crops (sorghum, millet) viable. Pygmies specialized rainforest adaptation; black farmers specialized savanna adaptation. Same human capability, different geographic optimization.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic habitat determines optimal subsistence technology. Pygmy hunting vs neighboring farmer agriculture reflects geographic habitat suitability. Physical differences (size, coloring) reflect local adaptation to environment, not biological incapacity. Population technology difference (hunting vs. farming) reflects geographic resource difference. Geographic determinism: habitat type determines technology type.
