Summary

Modern nomadic bands persist in resource-poor regions: Aboriginal Australians, Inuit, Tierra del Fuego Indians, northern boreal forest Indians. Nomadic hunter-gatherers remain primary adaptation despite agricultural success elsewhere. All humans lived in bands until ~40,000 years ago. Most humans still lived in bands as recently as 11,000 years ago. Bands lack institutions modern societies take for granted: no permanent settlements, no formal leadership, no armies, no police. Band organization reflects geographic resource distribution enabling/requiring continued nomadism.

Key Concepts

Band organization geography-determined optimal adaptation. Nomadism suits dispersed resources. Settlement suits concentrated resources. Agriculture prerequisite: domesticable plants geography-dependent. Resource-poor regions unable to support agriculture remain nomadic. Band institutions (egalitarian leadership, sharing, mobility) optimal for resource-sparse geography. Modern bands persist not through cultural conservatism but through geography-imposed resource constraints preventing agricultural transition. Institution type follows resource availability.
