Summary

Aboriginal Australian societies demonstrate internal variation in innovation receptivity, refuting the stereotype of Aboriginal cultural conservatism. Some Aboriginal groups adopted silversmithing, ranching, and modern transport technologies while continuing traditional dwellings. Tasmania shows extreme conservatism (stone tools persisted tens of thousands of years longer than Eurasia). Southeast Aboriginal fishing groups show receptivity to innovation. Internal variation within Aboriginal populations disproves continental ideology theory.

Key Concepts

Innovation receptivity varies by geography and economic factors, not cultural continuity. Geographic isolation (Tasmania) limits contact with innovations and reduces economic incentive for adoption. Trade access and economic opportunity determine technology adoption. Within-population variation exceeds between-continent differences. Stereotype of "conservative Aboriginal peoples" masks internal diversity of receptive and conservative societies. Technology adoption depends on geographic context enabling profitable application, not cultural ideology.
