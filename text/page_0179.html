Summary

Genetic analysis determines domestication origin: single vs. multiple independently. Widespread crops show identical mutations (single origin) or different mutations (multiple origins). Genetic examination of crop varieties reveals wild ancestor mutations. Identical mutations indicate diffusion from single origin. Different mutations indicate independent domestication in separate regions.

Key Concepts

Genetic signatures reveal domestication history. Mutation specificity tracks origin. Single origin domestication â†’ single mutation type. Multiple independent domestications â†’ multiple different mutations. Genetic analysis differentiates diffusion (single origin) from independent invention (multiple origins). Geography determines domestication origin clustering vs. dispersal.

Historical & Geographic Context

Crops widespread in prehistoric times examined genetically. Wheat: genetic analysis shows single origin (Fertile Crescent), spread via diffusion to Europe/Asia. Maize: multiple domestication centers suggested by genetic variation. Potato: South American origin, multiple domestication regions (Andean variation). Genetic evidence distinguishes diffusion vs. independent invention patterns.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic isolation determines domestication independence vs. diffusion. Isolated regions = independent domestication = multiple genetic mutations. Connected regions = diffusion = single genetic mutation spread. Genetic signatures geography-determined through geographic isolation/connection patterns. Geographic isolation enables independent domestication; geographic connectivity enables diffusion replacing independent domestication.
