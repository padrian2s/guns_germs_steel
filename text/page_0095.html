Summary

Archaeological identification of domestication: morphological differences between wild and domestic species. Domestic cattle smaller, chickens/apples larger, pea seed coats thinner, goat horns twisted differently. These morphological changes accumulate through generations, allowing archaeologists to identify domestication at dated sites.

Key Concepts

Archaeological evidence of domestication. Morphological changes distinguish domesticated from wild species. Dating domesticated remains allows temporal mapping of agricultural emergence. Geographic distribution of domestication sites reveals independent invention centers and diffusion patterns.

Historical & Geographic Context

Multiple independent agricultural origins visible archaeologically. Fertile Crescent domestication 10000 BCE. China 9000 BCE. Mesoamerica 5000 BCE. Each region's domesticated species reflect available wild flora/fauna. Archaeological record confirms geographic variation in agricultural emergence.

Connection to Main Thesis

Morphological evidence in archaeological record proves geographic determination of agriculture. Each region domesticated locally available species.
