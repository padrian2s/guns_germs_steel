Rezumat

Eastern U.S. wild crops possessed superior nutritional profiles (sumpweed 32% protein, 45% oil; sunflower 45-47% oil) compared to domesticated crops (wheat 8-14%, corn 9%). Yet domestication never occurred. Question: why? Answer lies in domesticability traits: these species lacked genetic mutations enabling efficient domestication despite nutritional advantages.

Concepte Cheie

Domesticability genetics distinct from nutritional content. High-nutrition wild species failed domestication due to genetics. Sumpweed, sunflower: seeds too small, plants difficult to cultivate, yields inadequate. Grains: large seeds, reliable growth, high yields. Genetic domesticability, not nutritional value, determines agricultural adoption.

Context Istoric È™i Geografic

Eastern U.S. wild flora: diverse edible species with superior nutrition compared to eventually-domesticated grains/corn. Yet maize, beans, squash emerged as staples. Sumpweed abandoned despite potential. Geography's available wild species included nutritious failures: high nutrition but poor domestication genetics. Geography determined what was domesticable, not what was optimal nutritionally.
