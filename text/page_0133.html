Summary

Archaeological evidence suggests ancient peoples explored virtually all useful wild plants and domesticated all worth domesticating. Yet some failures remain puzzling: domestication successes in one region but failures in another region with the same species. Cases demonstrate feasibility yet regional non-adoption requires explanation.

Key Concepts

Domestication feasibility vs. adoption. Species domesticable in region A but not adopted in region B. Not technology barrier (know-how spreads). Not feasibility barrier (proven in region A). Regional preference, wild productivity, or diffusion barriers explain differential adoption. Geographic factors other than domesticability determine adoption patterns.

Connection to Main Thesis

Domesticability necessary but insufficient for adoption. Geographic factors determine adoption: wild plant productivity (no adoption pressure), diffusion barriers (isolated regions), cultural factors (food preferences).
