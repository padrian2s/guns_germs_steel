Summary

Infectious disease origins from domesticated animals: rinderpest from cattle, other diseases from livestock species. 9,000 years of human-livestock intimacy (since domestication) enabled disease jumping to humans. Microbes evolve to exploit human-animal proximity. Geography determines domesticable animal availability: regions lacking domesticable animals lack livestock diseases epidemic diseases.

Key Concepts

Disease epidemiology geography-determined: depends on domesticable animal availability. Populations without domesticable animals developed without epidemic diseases. Populations with domesticable animals developed epidemic disease resistance through repeated exposure. Humans living at high population density due to agriculture enabled germs to jump species. Geographic advantages create multiple effects: agriculture enables population density; density enables epidemic disease; disease experience creates biological immunity.

Historical & Geographic Context

Fertile Crescent agriculture: domesticated cattle, sheep, goats, pigs - all disease reservoirs. Eurasia livestock emphasis created epidemic disease ecosystem. Americas lacked domesticable animals (no large mammals except llama/alpaca)â€”populations lacked epidemic disease exposure. Africa limited domesticable animals compared to Eurasia. Intimate human-livestock contact in agricultural societies: shared housing, food sources, feces contamination. Disease evolution: pathogens requiring human population density couldn't emerge in hunter-gatherer populations.

Connection to Main Thesis

Geographic distribution of domesticable animals determines epidemic disease distribution. Eurasia fauna enabled extensive livestock domestication creating epidemic disease reservoir. Americas fauna prevented livestock-based agriculture limiting epidemic disease development. Disease advantages created by geography: Eurasian immunity from childhood exposure to epidemic diseases became biological advantage in contact with Americas peoples lacking disease immunity.
